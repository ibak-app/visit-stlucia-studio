Currency & Money

Saint Lucia's currency is the Eastern Caribbean Dollar, pegged to the US dollar at a fixed rate since 1976. Understanding money here is simple once you know the basics.

EC$2.70 = US$1.00 (fixed peg)
EC$ Eastern Caribbean Dollar
USD Widely accepted
ECCB Eastern Caribbean Central Bank

The Eastern Caribbean Dollar (EC$) is the official currency of Saint Lucia and seven other Eastern Caribbean island nations. It has been pegged to the US dollar at EC$2.70 = US$1.00 since 1976 ‚Äî this rate never changes. This makes mental conversion simple: divide EC$ prices by 2.7 to get US$.

Quick examples:

  • EC$27 = US$10
  • EC$54 = US$20
  • EC$135 = US$50
  • EC$270 = US$100

When you see prices in both currencies, always check whether the exchange rate being applied is 2.70 or a slightly worse rate (2.60‚Äì2.65) as some vendors quote informally. The official rate is always 2.70.

US dollars are widely accepted at hotels, resorts, restaurants, tour operators, and most tourist-facing businesses throughout Saint Lucia. Many prices in tourist areas are quoted in US dollars.

However, local markets, small shops, minibuses, street food vendors, and village businesses typically prefer EC$. If you pay in USD at a local business, you may receive change in EC$ ‚Äî which is fine, but check the rate being applied.

Best approach: Have some EC$ for everyday local spending (food markets, minibuses, tips, small purchases) and keep USD or a card for bigger purchases at hotels and tour operators.

Visa and Mastercard are accepted at most hotels, restaurants, car rental companies, and larger tour operators. American Express acceptance is less reliable. Contactless payment is increasingly available at modern establishments.

Where cards don't work: Local markets, minibuses, small street vendors, many tour guides and gig economy operators, and some small guesthouses operate cash-only. Always have EC$ or USD cash available for these situations.

ATM advice: ATMs are available in Castries (Bank of Saint Lucia, First National Bank of St Lucia, RBC Royal Bank), Rodney Bay (near the marina), Vieux Fort, and Gros Islet. They dispense EC$. Notify your bank before travelling to avoid your card being blocked for overseas transactions. ATM fees can be significant ‚Äî try to withdraw larger amounts less frequently.

üí°

Practical Tip ‚Äî Currency Exchange

The best exchange rates are at banks and official exchange bureaux in Castries. Airport exchange kiosks offer worse rates. Hotels typically offer the worst exchange rates of all. If you're changing money, do it at a bank in town. For most visitors, simply withdrawing EC$ from an ATM on arrival is the most convenient and competitive option.

Electricity & Plugs

This is the one that catches most North American and some European visitors off guard. Get your adaptor sorted before you leave home.

‚ö°

UK-Type Plugs (Type G) at 220V/50Hz

Saint Lucia uses UK-style three-pin plugs (Type G) at 220 volts, 50Hz. If you're travelling from North America (where the standard is 110V / 60Hz with two flat pins), you need both a plug adaptor AND a voltage converter for devices that are not dual-voltage. If you're from continental Europe (two round pins, 220V), you need a plug adaptor but should be fine on voltage. UK visitors need nothing at all.

Most modern electronics are dual-voltage ‚Äî they can handle both 110V and 220V automatically. Look for the label on the device itself (or its charging brick): it will say "Input: 100‚Äì240V" if it's dual-voltage. This includes most laptop chargers, phone chargers, camera chargers, and USB adaptors.

Devices that are NOT dual-voltage and WILL be damaged at 220V without a converter: older hair dryers, some curling irons/straighteners, some travel appliances. Check before you go.

The simplest solution: buy a universal travel adaptor with USB ports. It handles the plug shape issue and lets you charge USB devices without worrying about voltage (the USB converters inside are dual-voltage). For high-wattage devices (hair dryers), buy a 220V travel hair dryer rather than packing a converter ‚Äî they're cheaper and lighter.

Most hotels catering to North American guests have a mix of socket types ‚Äî some rooms include US-style sockets (110V) in addition to UK-style ones. High-end all-inclusive resorts often have universal socket adaptors in rooms or at the front desk on loan. If in doubt, call ahead and ask.

Boutique guesthouses and smaller properties may have strictly UK-type sockets throughout. Don't assume and be left without a charge for your phone on the first night ‚Äî pack an adaptor regardless.

Language

English is the official language and there's no language barrier for English-speaking visitors. But understanding Kw√©y√≤l opens a window into the real Saint Lucia.

Saint Lucia's official language is English, spoken in education, government, media, and business. All signage is in English. You will not struggle to communicate anywhere on the island as an English speaker.

Kw√©y√≤l (also spelled Creole or Patois) is a French-based Creole language spoken widely in informal settings ‚Äî at home, in markets, between friends, and in rural communities. It evolved among enslaved Africans in the French Caribbean, blending African grammatical structures with French vocabulary. A significant number of older Saint Lucians speak Kw√©y√≤l as their primary language at home, though nearly everyone also speaks English.

A few phrases go a very long way in showing respect for local culture:

  • Bonjou ‚Äî Good morning (pronounced "bon-zhoo")
  • Bonswa ‚Äî Good afternoon / Good evening
  • Oui / Non ‚Äî Yes / No
  • M√®si ‚Äî Thank you
  • Sa ka f√®t? ‚Äî What's happening? / How are you?
  • Tout bagay ka mach√© ‚Äî Everything is fine (literally "everything is walking")
  • Kouman w r√©l√©? ‚Äî What is your name?

Saint Lucians absolutely appreciate when visitors make the effort to use even a word or two of Kw√©y√≤l. It immediately marks you as someone interested in more than just the beach, and often opens up much warmer and more genuine conversations.

Saint Lucian English accent: The English spoken in Saint Lucia has a distinctive Caribbean musicality that some visitors initially find takes a moment to tune into. The rhythm and intonation are different from North American or British English. Within a day or two, your ear adjusts completely and you'll find it entirely clear ‚Äî and probably the most pleasant English accent you've ever heard.

Simple and consistent ‚Äî Saint Lucia does not observe daylight saving time.

Visas & Entry Requirements

Most visitors to Saint Lucia do not need a visa. Entry is straightforward, but a few things are worth knowing.

‚úàÔ∏è

Visa-Free for Most Nationalities

Citizens of the UK, USA, Canada, Australia, the EU, most Commonwealth countries, and many others can enter Saint Lucia visa-free for tourism stays of up to 6 weeks (42 days). No advance application is needed ‚Äî you receive your entry stamp on arrival. This covers the vast majority of leisure visitors.

  • Valid passport ‚Äî must be valid for the duration of your stay. Many countries require 6 months validity beyond your travel dates ‚Äî check your country's specific requirement.
  • Return or onward ticket ‚Äî immigration officers may ask to see proof that you're leaving. Have your return flight details accessible.
  • Proof of accommodation ‚Äî hotel booking confirmation or invitation letter if staying with friends/family.
  • Sufficient funds ‚Äî you may be asked to demonstrate you have funds for your stay. Credit cards or bank statements work.
  • Completed arrival card ‚Äî distributed on the aircraft or available at the airport. Fill this out before reaching immigration.

If you wish to stay beyond the initial 6-week tourist allowance, you can apply for an extension at the Immigration Department in Castries. Extensions are typically granted for further periods of up to 6 weeks at a time for a fee.

Working in Saint Lucia on a tourist visa is not permitted. If you intend to work, you need a work permit, which must be applied for by your employer or sponsor before you arrive. Remote work on a tourist visa exists in a legal grey area ‚Äî there is no specific digital nomad visa as of 2026, though this is an area several Caribbean nations are actively developing.

For residency, long-term stays, or CBI (Citizenship by Investment) enquiries, see the full guide at stlucia.studio.

Saint Lucia follows CARICOM customs rules. Standard allowances include: 1 litre of spirits, 200 cigarettes (or equivalent tobacco), and a reasonable quantity of personal effects and gifts. Agricultural products (fresh fruit, vegetables, meat, plants) may be subject to inspection or restrictions ‚Äî check CARICOM regulations if you're planning to bring food items.

Declare cash amounts over US$10,000 (or equivalent). Failure to declare is a serious offence. Prescription medication should be in original packaging with a copy of the prescription.

SIM Cards & Mobile Coverage

Getting connected in Saint Lucia is easy and cheap. Don't rely on roaming ‚Äî it's almost always overpriced.

Flow (C&W Communications) is Saint Lucia's largest telecommunications network. It offers the most extensive coverage across the island, including in rural and mountainous areas. Flow SIM cards are available at the airport, at their stores in Castries and Rodney Bay, and at various retail outlets.

A Flow starter SIM pack costs approximately EC$40 (~US$15) and typically includes initial data credit. Top-up data packages range from EC$30 (~US$11) for 1GB to EC$150+ (~US$56) for larger bundles. Coverage is 4G LTE in urban areas and major tourist destinations, with 3G in more remote areas.

Digicel is Flow's main competitor and offers competitive data packages with good coverage in tourist areas and along the main roads. If you already use Digicel in another Caribbean country, your account may work on roaming ‚Äî check before you travel.

Digicel SIMs are also available at the airport and in Castries. The same approximate pricing applies (EC$40 starter pack, top-up packages available).

Coverage comparison: Flow generally has better coverage in the southern end of the island and in more rural areas. In the main tourist zones (Rodney Bay, Castries, Soufriere, Gros Islet), both networks perform comparably.

Wi-Fi is available at virtually all hotels and resorts, most restaurants in tourist areas, and the main shopping centres in Castries and Rodney Bay. Quality varies from excellent (resort-grade fibre) to frustratingly slow (oversubscribed shared connections).

Do not rely on hotel Wi-Fi for video calls or large downloads during busy periods (breakfast time, evenings). A local SIM for mobile data backup is well worth the cost.

Free public Wi-Fi hotspots exist at Derek Walcott Square in Castries and at some government facilities. Connection quality is variable.

All-Inclusive Hotels and Tipping

Many all-inclusive resorts have a no-tipping policy ‚Äî tips are factored into the overall rate and shared among all staff. Check your resort's policy on arrival. Safety

Saint Lucia is a generally safe Caribbean destination. Understanding the real picture ‚Äî not the overstated warnings, not the naively optimistic view ‚Äî helps you travel confidently.

The honest assessment: Saint Lucia is safe for tourists exercising ordinary common sense. The vast majority of visitors have entirely incident-free trips. Tourist areas ‚Äî Rodney Bay, Castries waterfront, Pigeon Island, Marigot Bay, Soufriere ‚Äî are well-visited and generally safe during daylight hours and in the early evening.

Some areas of Castries (particularly inland residential areas away from the tourist waterfront) experience higher rates of petty crime. Avoid walking through unfamiliar areas of Castries at night. The Derek Walcott Square area and main commercial streets are generally fine during the day.

Some beach areas at night are not well-lit and are best avoided. Stick to the well-trafficked beach strips (Reduit Beach in Rodney Bay) in the evenings. Remote beaches are beautiful during the day ‚Äî avoid them after dark.

Vieux Fort has a reputation for being rougher than the north of the island. If visiting for Anse de Sables beach (worth it for wind sports), go during the day and be more vigilant with belongings than you might be in Rodney Bay.

The main safety concern for tourists in Saint Lucia is opportunistic petty theft ‚Äî left-unattended belongings on beaches, phones left on restaurant tables, bags in rental cars. These are easily preventable with simple habits:

  • Never leave valuables on the beach unattended, even briefly
  • Don't leave bags or phones visible in parked rental cars
  • Use your hotel safe for passport, extra cash, and valuables
  • Be aware of your surroundings in busy markets and on minibuses
  • Don't wear expensive jewellery or display expensive cameras/phones unnecessarily

Ocean safety: The Atlantic side of the island has strong currents and is unsuitable for casual swimming. The west (Caribbean) coast is calmer. Heed beach flags. Never swim alone in remote areas.

Road safety: Saint Lucia drives on the left. Roads in tourist areas are generally good; secondary roads can be narrow, winding, and poorly maintained. Mountain roads around Soufriere are particularly challenging ‚Äî take them slowly. Minibus drivers are often... enthusiastic. Road accidents are the single biggest cause of tourist injury in Saint Lucia, so drive carefully and always wear a seatbelt.

Hiking: Don't hike without a guide. Don't hike alone. Tell your hotel where you're going and when you expect to return. Have water, sun protection, and a phone (offline maps downloaded).

Health

Saint Lucia has decent medical facilities by Caribbean standards, and most health risks are easily preventable.

Travel insurance with medical cover is essential. While public healthcare exists, private clinics (which can provide faster and higher-quality care for tourists) charge fees upfront. Medical repatriation from Saint Lucia to North America or Europe is very expensive without insurance. Any comprehensive travel insurance policy should include at minimum: medical emergency cover, emergency repatriation, and trip cancellation. Don't leave home without it.

Dengue fever is present in Saint Lucia and the wider Caribbean. It's transmitted by mosquitoes that bite during the day (Aedes aegypti), so sunscreen and bug spray should be applied simultaneously during outdoor activities. Symptoms include high fever, severe headache, joint and muscle pain, and rash. If you develop these symptoms within 2 weeks of returning home, tell your doctor you visited the Caribbean.

Zika virus is present but at low transmission levels. Pregnant women or those planning pregnancy should consult their doctor before travelling. Malaria is not present in Saint Lucia.

Prevention: Use DEET or picaridin-based insect repellent. Wear long sleeves and trousers at dawn and dusk. Use mosquito nets if your accommodation doesn't have screened windows.

Saint Lucia has two main hospitals:

  • Victoria Hospital ‚Äî Castries (main public hospital). Handles most emergencies. Basic by North American/European standards but functional for serious injuries and illnesses.
  • St. Jude Hospital ‚Äî Vieux Fort. Completed November 2025 at a cost of US$75 million, this new facility significantly upgraded healthcare capacity in the south of the island.

Private medical clinics in Rodney Bay and Castries provide faster service for non-emergency care. Bring copies of any prescription medications with the generic drug name (not just the brand name) in case you need a local equivalent. Most common medications are available at pharmacies in Castries and Rodney Bay.

No specific vaccinations are required for entry to Saint Lucia (unless arriving from a yellow fever endemic country, in which case a yellow fever certificate is required). Recommended vaccinations for most travellers include:

  • Routine vaccines (MMR, diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis, chickenpox, flu)
  • Hepatitis A (food and water borne ‚Äî recommended)
  • Hepatitis B (if staying long-term or in clinical settings)
  • Typhoid (if you'll be eating in local restaurants and markets ‚Äî recommended)

Consult your doctor or a travel health clinic 4‚Äì6 weeks before travel for personalised advice.

Best Time to Visit

Saint Lucia has no bad time to visit ‚Äî but knowing the difference between the seasons helps you make the right call for your priorities.

The classic "high season" for Caribbean travel. Lower humidity, drier weather, slightly cooler temperatures (25‚Äì28¬∞C / 77‚Äì82¬∞F). The trade winds are constant and cooling. You're almost guaranteed sunshine.

The downsides: Accommodation prices are 30‚Äì50% higher than low season. Popular resorts sell out months ahead. Beaches are busier. Some tours and activities book out. If visiting at Christmas and New Year, budget for significant price premiums and book 6+ months ahead.

Best for: Honeymooners, first-time visitors wanting guaranteed sun, those who prefer the social buzz of a busier island.

Officially hurricane season, but Saint Lucia's southerly position in the Eastern Caribbean means it very rarely gets a direct hurricane hit. What it does get is afternoon rain showers, higher humidity, lush green landscapes, and significantly lower prices.

The real picture: The majority of days even in the low season are sunny and warm (28‚Äì32¬∞C / 82‚Äì90¬∞F). Rainfall typically comes in short, intense bursts ‚Äî often in the afternoon ‚Äî rather than all-day grey weather. The island turns an extraordinary shade of green. You'll often have beaches almost to yourself.

Savings: Hotel prices drop 30‚Äì50%. All-inclusive packages offer genuine value. Flights are cheaper. Activities are less crowded.

Best for: Budget-conscious travellers, repeat visitors, nature lovers, those who prefer a quieter, more authentic experience, and festival-goers (Jazz in May, Carnival in June‚ÄìJuly).

Honest Recommendation

If budget is not a concern and you want guaranteed great weather with lively social scenes, go December‚ÄìMarch. If you want great value, lush scenery, fewer crowds, and you don't mind the occasional afternoon shower, May‚ÄìNovember (outside of peak festival weeks) offers exceptional value. October ‚Äî Jounen Kw√©y√≤l season ‚Äî is a personal favourite: excellent weather, authentic local culture, and prices that are half what they'd be in January.

Packing List

What to bring, what to leave behind, and what you can buy when you get there.

  • Lightweight clothing ‚Äî quick-dry fabrics. Cotton is comfortable but heavy when wet. Technical fabrics or linen are ideal.
  • Swimwear ‚Äî two or three sets so there's always a dry one available
  • Light cover-up ‚Äî for going from beach to restaurant or entering churches/inland areas. Shoulders covered is appropriate for many situations.
  • Light rain jacket or packable poncho ‚Äî compact and often needed, especially if hiking or on a boat tour
  • One smart-casual outfit ‚Äî for nicer restaurants (some require long trousers and collared shirts for men; many don't)
  • Flip-flops or sandals ‚Äî for beach and casual wear
  • Comfortable walking shoes ‚Äî for town, markets, and casual walks
  • Hiking shoes or trail runners ‚Äî essential if you're planning the Gros Piton hike or any nature trails
  • Reef-safe sunscreen (SPF 50+) ‚Äî important for both your skin and the reefs. Bring enough from home; reef-safe options are available locally but limited in selection and more expensive.
  • Insect repellent ‚Äî DEET or picaridin-based. Available locally but bring some to have immediately on arrival.
  • After-sun lotion ‚Äî you will underestimate the sun on your first day. Have it ready.
  • Prescription medications ‚Äî bring your full supply plus a few extra days, in original packaging with a copy of your prescription.
  • Basic first aid kit ‚Äî plasters, antiseptic, motion sickness tablets (roads and boat trips warrant it), rehydration salts.
  • Hand sanitiser ‚Äî for street food situations and when handwashing isn't available.
  • UK-type plug adaptor (Type G) ‚Äî absolutely essential for North Americans and most Europeans
  • Portable power bank ‚Äî useful for long days out, especially if using maps and photography
  • Waterproof phone pouch ‚Äî for boat trips, beach snorkelling, and rainy days
  • Unlocked phone ‚Äî to use with a local SIM card (check your phone is unlocked before you travel)
  • Offline maps downloaded ‚Äî Google Maps or Maps.me with Saint Lucia downloaded for offline use. Mobile coverage can be patchy in rural areas.
  • Camera with dry bag ‚Äî the scenery demands it. Keep electronics dry.
  • Expensive jewellery ‚Äî leave it home. Not worth the security risk or the worry.
  • Heavy hiking boots ‚Äî trail runners are lighter and equally good for Gros Piton
  • Formal business attire ‚Äî unless you're genuinely attending business meetings. Smart-casual covers all scenarios.
  • Your own snorkel gear ‚Äî rental is available cheaply at all major snorkel beaches. Only bring your own if you specifically prefer your mask fit.
  • Hair dryer (non-dual voltage) ‚Äî it will be fried at 220V. Buy a 220V travel dryer or use what your hotel provides.
