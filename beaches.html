All Beaches Are Public

Saint Lucia law guarantees public access to all beaches. Even beaches fronted by five-star resorts are legally accessible to everyone. What resorts can restrict is access through their private property â€” but you can always enter via a public road or from the water.

Saint Lucia's most popular beach, and deservedly so. A long crescent of golden-brown sand fringed with palm trees, with calm Caribbean water and a lively strip of beach bars, restaurants and water sports operators. Rodney Bay is the social hub of Saint Lucia â€” this is where the action is.

A remote, dark-sand bay with one of the Caribbean's most spectacular snorkelling reefs just metres from shore. The reef drops gradually to deeper water, offering easy access for beginners and rewarding dives for experienced snorkellers. The Piton peaks loom dramatically behind the beach.

Marine life: Sea turtles, parrotfish, angelfish, lionfish, squid, moray eels and over 150 fish species have been recorded here. The coral is in genuinely good condition by Caribbean standards.

Arguably the most photographed beach in the Caribbean. Nestled in the valley between Gros Piton and Petit Piton, with white volcanic sand (imported and treated) and crystal-clear water. The Viceroy Sugar Beach Resort sits here, but the beach is accessible to all.

Access: The Sugar Beach resort manages beach chair rentals but cannot restrict access to the beach itself. Access by road (very rough track from Soufriere) or by water taxi from Soufriere town (~EC$30). Most visitors arrive via the resort or on catamaran tours that anchor in the bay.

Underwater: A healthy reef runs along the rocky headlands on each side of the bay. Visibility is excellent in calm conditions.

Photography: Shoot from the water looking back at the beach for the iconic Piton-framing shot.

Beach Etiquette & Access

Understanding how beaches work in Saint Lucia will help you avoid awkward situations.

All beaches in Saint Lucia are legally public up to the high water mark. Resorts cannot legally deny you access to a beach, but they can â€” and do â€” restrict access through their private grounds.

This means you can walk along the beach past resort sun loungers without issue, but you may need to enter by boat or via a public road rather than through the resort's lobby.

On public beaches like Reduit, independent vendors rent chairs and umbrellas for EC$20â€“40 per day. On resort beaches, chairs are typically for guests only â€” though at some resorts you can buy a day pass to use their facilities.

If someone approaches you selling beach time (chairs, braiding, fruit) and you're not interested, a polite firm "no thank you" is respected. Don't feel pressured.

Topless sunbathing is not generally practised in Saint Lucia and is not considered culturally appropriate on public beaches. Some private resort pools or secluded coves may be more relaxed, but exercise good judgement â€” if no locals are doing it, you probably shouldn't be either.

Full nudity in public is technically illegal.

Please use reef-safe sunscreen â€” particularly at snorkelling beaches like Anse Chastanet and Sugar Beach. Chemical sunscreens containing oxybenzone and octinoxate damage coral reefs significantly. Look for mineral sunscreens with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide.

Saint Lucia hasn't yet legislated on sunscreen as some jurisdictions have, but it's simply the responsible choice. The reefs here are in better shape than most of the Caribbean â€” let's keep them that way.
